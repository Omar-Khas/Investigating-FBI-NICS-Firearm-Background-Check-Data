Since 1998, the National Instant Criminal Background Check System has screened firearm transactions across all 50 states. This investigation cross-references 12,485 NICS records with U.S. Census data to ask: who buys guns, where, when — and why do the numbers keep climbing?

Key Findings
Geographic

Kentucky dominated — in every year

29.7M total

Kentucky topped every other state in both 2010 (211,261 checks) and 2016 (363,085 checks). Confirmed using idxmax() on the merged dataset. The gap is partly explained by a state policy of re-running NICS checks on existing permit holders every month — significantly inflating raw totals vs. other states.

Seasonal

December is always the busiest month

29,025 avg

December averaged 29,025 checks per state — the highest of any month in the dataset. This is nearly 60% more than the April low (18,112). The pattern holds consistently across all years, pointing to holiday gift-buying and year-end permit windows as the primary drivers.

Demographic

Female populations slightly higher in top-sales states

50.7% ♀ KY

Kentucky (highest sales, 2016) was 50.7% female. Hawaii (lowest sales, 1,565 checks) was 49.8% female. The notebook's pie charts reveal that in both 2010 and 2016, states with the most checks had slightly higher female percentages — though no causal claim can be made without deeper analysis.

Research Questions Answered
Q1: Which state had the highest gun sales in 2010 and 2016?
Kentucky in both years. In April 2010: 211,261 total checks. In July 2016: 363,085 — a 72% increase. Confirmed using idxmax() on the merged gun + census DataFrame. Even accounting for Kentucky's permit-recheck policy, no other state comes close.
Q2: Which state saw the biggest increase 2010–2016?
Kentucky again — with the largest absolute difference when plotting totals_16 - totals_10 for all states. Notably, Utah experienced the most significant decrease over the same period, suggesting state-level policy changes rather than a uniform national trend.
Q3: Is there a racial pattern in firearm acquisition across states?
Waffle chart analysis showed that Kentucky (88% White population) had the highest checks, while Hawaii (25.8% White, 37.7% Asian) had the lowest (1,565 in 2016). The pattern shows correlation — but not causation. Many other factors (rural vs urban, policy, culture) likely explain the disparity.
Q4: Does veteran population correlate with gun sales?
A scatter plot across all 50 states for 2011–2015 showed a positive correlation of 0.52 between veteran population and firearm background checks. This is a moderately strong signal computed using np.corrcoef(). States with more veterans tend to have more checks, though causality was not established.

Methodology & Limitations

Two datasets were used: FBI NICS monthly records (1998–2017, 12,485 rows × 27 columns) and U.S. Census Bureau data (50 states, estimates for 2010, 2011–2015, and 2016). The gun data had significant NaN values — permit_recheck alone had 11,385 missing rows — handled using forward-fill. Census data required transposing (rows were states, columns were facts), stripping commas and % signs from strings, and converting to numeric types. Cross-referencing used inner joins on state names. Visualizations included bar charts, scatter plots, pie charts, and PyWaffle waffle charts. Key caveat from the conclusions: background checks ≠ firearms sold. A single check can cover multiple firearms, and some states re-run checks on existing permit holders monthly, inflating totals. Further data (median salary, education per racial group, economic status of veteran populations) would be needed for more conclusive findings.
